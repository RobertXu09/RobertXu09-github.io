At this point in the class, I’ve worked a bit with Photoshop and other photo-editing tools. I’ve also presented my Pecha Kucha, and I’ve seen a few other presentations, so I have a better understanding of design now. For the assignments I’ve done so far in the photo-editing unit, I have paid attention to learning multiple softwares, applying concepts and techniques that are ubiquitous to photo-editing no matter the software, and utilizing my own creativity to create content I want to make.

For the first assignment of the unit, I parodied an old silverware ad by replacing some of the silverware with baby utensils. The main purpose of this task was for me to familiarize myself with using Photoshop and its tools. I’ve never used Photoshop before, but I’ve used Gimp in the past to make very simple Facebook event banners and I’ve used some drawing softwares to make art and comics. Coming in, I was familiar with layers and the pencil and select tools, but that’s about it. To do this assignment in Photoshop, I used some of Photoshop’s many functions, including the clone stamp tool. I also played around with the RGB channels and filters to see if I wanted to make things grayscale or not. It was an eye-opening task -- I realized that Photoshop is a very dense software, and learning to use all of its functions would be time-consuming, but powerful.

The next assignment of the unit was the genre-mixing movie poster. I was tasked to present a horror movie as a documentary. Before even using any software, I analyzed different documentary posters and compared them to horror movie posters. Documentaries are presented in a much more grandiose and inspirational manner than I was expecting. Many seem to focus on the event or person of interest and create an attention grabbing phrase to make learning information about the documentary’s focus dramatic or interesting. Most of the fonts used are clear and professional, as opposed to the round, bubbly comedic fonts or the sometimes gory fonts of some horror movies. For my horror movie turned documentary, I chose Steven King’s “The Shining”. To apply some of the documentary-esque elements I learned about, I chose a professional-looking serif font, a dramatic scene capturing my subject interest, and added a generic but intriguing tagline to foster curiosity about how a man is related to the paranormal.

For assignment number three, I made a storyboard. For this task, I just used the Pecha Kucha that I already presented, and wrote out my notes for my slides in the format of a storyboard. To make my template, I created a layer in Gimp of just a box for me to have an idea of what to put on a slide, and I had some lines to write my thoughts for what I would say during my presentation. For each scene of the storyboard, I created a new layer to put my content on, keeping the template visible and underneath.

I really enjoyed the last assignment of the unit - the company logo. It is something I had thought about already. I always wondered what would make a good concise logo, and how some logos like Nike’s swoosh seem so fitting but abstract and simple. I also have always wanted to make a webcomic, and I would want a logo for that project. I decided to use Krita for this project, an open-source art software that emphasizes tools that are more drawing focused. It is one of the software’s that I used to familiarize myself with using layers. For my logo, I tried to imagine the vibe I wanted my comic to promote, which is derpy, cute, and silly. I then used a soft color palette, big, round shapes, and a fun, doodle-esque font. I think if I were to edit it, I would try something even simpler and more abstract for a logo.

For my website, I most recently implemented the checklist. For my checklist, I used a table element, but my table looked condensed and cramped. To solve this, I googled some css to create borders and center the text within the table. As for the rest of the site, I decided I am actually pretty happy with the design. It fits my art style, including my logo, and the text fits on the site better than I expected. I feared adding paragraphs would make the site look messy, but centering the text and constricting the width of the paragraph actually makes the content still look pretty clear. I cared mostly about clarity on my site, and in my opinion my site still preserves my personality while maintaining clarity.
